📊 Statistics

Hypothesis testing

α (alpha)Significance level — the pre-set false-positive rate: \( P(\text{reject } H_0 \mid H_0\ \text{true}) \) = Type I error. Typically 0.05.
β (beta)False-negative rate: \( P(\text{fail to reject } H_0 \mid H_0\ \text{false}) \) = Type II error. Power \( = 1 - \beta \). Typically β = 0.20 → 80% power.
z-testCompares a mean when the population SD (σ) is known or n is large; normal distribution. \( Z = \dfrac{\bar X - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \)
X̄ = sample mean, μ = population mean, σ = population SD, n = sample size. Also the standard test for proportions.
t-testCompares a mean when the population SD is unknown (estimated by sample SD s) / small n; t-distribution (fatter tails). \( t = \dfrac{\bar X - \mu}{s/\sqrt{n}} \), df = \( n-1 \)
Same shape as z, but with sample SD s in place of σ.
BootstrapResample with replacement many times, recompute the statistic → empirical sampling distribution → CI / SE with no distributional assumption. Estimates uncertainty of a median / ratio / skewed metric. (A Monte Carlo method.)
Permutation testShuffle the group labels many times to build the null distribution of the difference → p-value = fraction as extreme as observed. Tests a difference non-parametrically; good for small samples / non-normal data.
🎲 Probability

Bayes' theorem

Bayes' theorem\( P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(B \mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)} \) — updates the probability of hypothesis A given evidence B. Denominator: \( P(B) = P(B\mid A)P(A) + P(B\mid \neg A)P(\neg A) \). Interview trick: reason with natural frequencies (counts), not the formula.

Distributions

BernoulliSingle yes/no trial · param p · mean p, var p(1−p). One transaction: fraud or not.
Binomial# successes in n independent trials · params n, p · mean np, var np(1−p). # conversions out of N visitors — every conversion-rate A/B test.
Poisson# rare events in a fixed interval · param λ (rate) · mean = var = λ. Fraud events per day. If over-dispersed (var > mean) → Negative Binomial.
NormalContinuous, symmetric; sums/means via CLT · params μ, σ² · mean μ, var σ². Sampling distribution of a mean.
ExponentialTime between Poisson events · param λ · mean 1/λ, var 1/λ². Time between fraud attempts.
UniformAll outcomes equally likely · params a, b · mean (a+b)/2. Random assignment / baselines.
Links: Binomial = sum of n Bernoullis · Binomial ≈ Poisson when n large & p small (λ = np) · Poisson counts ↔ Exponential inter-arrival times.
🧪 Experimentation

Design & variance reduction

Factorial designTest multiple factors at once (e.g. 2×2) to get main effects + interactions. More efficient than one-at-a-time; the only way to catch interaction effects. Fractional = a subset of combos.
Hierarchical modelMultilevel / mixed-effects for nested data (users in cities in countries); partial pooling shrinks small-group estimates toward the global mean. Cluster-randomized / nested data → accounts for within-group correlation; stabilizes small segments.
CUPEDVariance reduction using pre-experiment data as a covariate → tighter CIs / more power at the same n. Regress out pre-period behavior before comparing groups.

Pitfalls & biases

Simpson's paradoxAn aggregate trend reverses when you split by a subgroup whose mix differs across groups (a confounder). Trust the segmented view — and in a randomized test it signals broken randomization.
Self-selection biasRespondents/participants differ systematically from non-participants; voluntary samples over-represent strong opinions (a.k.a. non-response / voluntary response bias). Fix: representative random sampling, or use behavioral data that covers everyone.
Interference (SUTVA violation)One unit's treatment affects another unit's outcome (social, marketplace, messaging) → contaminates control and dilutes / underestimates the measured effect. Fix: cluster / geo / graph randomization so effects stay within an arm.
Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM)Observed split ≠ planned split (e.g. 52/48 vs 50/50) before any effect. Signals broken randomization or biased logging. Invalidates the test — check with a chi-square test, then debug before trusting any result.

Causal inference (no A/B test)

Diff-in-Diff (DiD)Compare the before→after change in a treated group vs. a control group. Assumes parallel trends. Feature launched in one region at a known date.
Reg. discontinuity (RDD)Treatment assigned by a threshold; compare units just above vs. just below the cutoff. Eligibility cutoffs — strong local causal estimate.
Instrumental var. (IV)An instrument Z affects treatment X but touches outcome Y only through X → beats unobserved confounding. Randomized encouragement as instrument for adoption. Valid instruments are rare.
Propensity matching (PSM)Model P(treated | covariates), match treated to similar untreated units. Balances observed confounders only.
Synthetic controlWeighted blend of controls that mimics the treated unit's pre-period trajectory, then compare after. Single treated market, many candidate controls.
Double MLML to control for high-dimensional confounders while keeping a valid treatment-effect estimate (orthogonalization + cross-fitting). Many messy confounders.
CausalImpactGoogle's Bayesian structural time-series — models the counterfactual from control series to estimate an intervention's effect. Launch impact from time series, no randomization.
Selector: threshold → RDD · clean before/after + control → DiD · single market + time series → synthetic control / CausalImpact · valid instrument → IV · observed confounders → PSM / double ML.
🤖 Modeling

Classification metrics

Precision\( \dfrac{TP}{TP+FP} \) — of everything we flagged positive, how much truly is. "How often we cry wolf."
Recall\( \dfrac{TP}{TP+FN} \) — of all actual positives, how much we caught (a.k.a. sensitivity, TPR). "How much we miss."
PR-AUCArea under the precision-recall curve across thresholds. Best summary for imbalanced / rare-positive problems.
ROC-AUCArea under the ROC curve (TPR vs. FPR). = P(model ranks a random positive above a random negative). 0.5 = random, 1.0 = perfect. Can look optimistic under heavy imbalance → prefer PR-AUC there.

Regression metrics

RMSE\( \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{n}\sum (y-\hat y)^2} \) — root mean squared error. Same units as target. Range [0, ∞), 0 = perfect. Penalizes large errors most (outlier-sensitive).
MAE\( \dfrac{1}{n}\sum |y-\hat y| \) — mean absolute error. Same units. Range [0, ∞), 0 = perfect. Robust to outliers.
MAPE\( \dfrac{1}{n}\sum \left|\dfrac{y-\hat y}{y}\right| \) — mean absolute % error. Scale-free. Range [0, ∞) as a % (can exceed 100%), 0 = perfect. Blows up when y is near 0.
R²\( 1 - \dfrac{SS_{\text{res}}}{SS_{\text{tot}}} \) — share of variance explained. Range (−∞, 1]: 1 = perfect, 0 = no better than predicting the mean, negative = worse than the mean. Pair with an error metric — never report alone.

Methods

ClusteringUnsupervised segmentation by similarity. k-means (choose k via elbow/silhouette), hierarchical (dendrogram, no preset k), DBSCAN (density, finds outliers). Standardize features → choose k → profile clusters into actionable personas.
Monte CarloEstimate hard quantities by repeated random simulation (law of large numbers → empirical average converges to truth). Uncertainty with no closed form, power for complex designs, tail-risk / fraud loss distributions. Bootstrap is a Monte Carlo method.
Full model ladder (linear/logistic, trees, RF, gradient boosting, neural nets, recommenders) → see the 🎴 Flashcards.

Validation & leakage

Strategy vs. metricValidation strategy (holdout / CV / walk-forward) = HOW you split data to estimate generalization. Metric (precision, PR-AUC, RMSE…) = WHAT you compute on the held-out slice. Always used together.
HoldoutOne train/test split. Simple, cheap; fine for large data. Higher-variance estimate.
k-fold CVRotate through k train/val splits, average. Best for limited data (stable estimate) & tuning. Cost: k× training.
Stratified k-foldKeeps class balance in each fold — essential for imbalanced data (fraud).
Walk-forwardTrain on past → validate on next period → roll forward. Temporal data only; never random k-fold on time-ordered data (lookahead leakage).
⚠️ Leakage trapsTarget/feature leakage (a feature encodes the outcome) · temporal (future→past) · preprocessing (fit scaler/encoder on full data — fit on train only) · group (same user in train & test → group CV) · tuning on the test set (→ nested CV).
🔍 Applied analysis

Measurement — metric types

North-starThe single top metric capturing core user value + business health. One per product; everything ladders up to it.
Driver / inputMore actionable levers that move the north star. e.g. fewer checkout steps → conversion.
GuardrailMetrics that must not get worse while you optimize the target. Latency, uninstalls, false-positive rate, retention.
FeatureA specific feature's adoption + success, measured locally. Carousel CTR, feature retention.
Counter / vanityBeware gameable / misleading metrics (raw volume, clicks). Pair with a quality counter-metric; prefer durable value (retained installs).

Approach — C · H · I · R

ClarifyRestate the question, define the metric, scope it, state assumptions.
HypothesizeA structured (MECE) space of causes — real vs. artifact, internal vs. external, by segment.
InvestigateTie each hypothesis to a check; prioritize by cost-to-rule-out (cheapest first).
RecommendLeading story, the decision / next step, and what you'd monitor.
